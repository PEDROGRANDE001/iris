# The Demise of Penrod's Fort Lauderdale & The Pivot to Miami Beach

## Penrod's Fort Lauderdale — The Setup

Penrod's Beach Club in Fort Lauderdale wasn't a standalone building — it was located inside the **Sheraton Yankee Trader** hotel on the beachfront. Jack made a deal with the hotel's owner, **Bob Gill**: $100,000 per year for the lobby space to create Penrod's. In the first year alone, they each pocketed **$1 million**.

To fill the place, Jack deployed the same aggressive marketing instincts he'd honed at McDonald's:
- Personally visited **129 college campuses**
- Rented **metro cars** to create spring break party trains bringing students down to Fort Lauderdale
- Grew the spring break crowd from **100,000 to 500,000** visitors
- At its peak, averaged **2,000 people per day** during spring break
- The volume actually became a problem — Jack bought one of the largest hotels in **Daytona Beach** just to spread the overflow

### The Business Model
- $5 plastic cup = unlimited drinks all day
- Pasta buffet (nobody remembered to eat)
- Multiple venues under one roof: disco bar, country-western saloon, pool area, and eventually The Underground (alternative music basement club)
- Revenue from sheer volume — hundreds of thousands of college kids spending every dollar they had

## The End of Fort Lauderdale (1985–1988)

The Fort Lauderdale location didn't close with a single event. It died from two converging forces:

### Force 1: The City Crackdown (1985–1987)
- 1985: 2,500+ arrests, new restrictive laws, drinking age raised from 19 to 21
- 1986: Year-round beach drinking ban, capacity/fire code enforcement, arrests doubled
- 1987: Mayor Bob Cox on Good Morning America saying the city could "use far less" spring break visitors
- Road construction tore up the beachfront, physically cutting off access
- Businesses along the Strip began closing in waves — "a tsunami of endings"

### Force 2: Jack Had Already Found the Next Thing
- **1984**: City of Miami Beach approached Jack to help revitalize South Beach
- Jack **declined the first time** — he drove down Ocean Drive, saw drug addicts on the streets, paraphernalia on the beach. Too dangerous. He said no.
- **1985**: Miami Beach came back a second time. Promised him "anything he wanted."
- Jack and **Lucia** (his marketing director, later his wife) wrote a **list of 200 demands**
- They negotiated: control of the beach from **1st to 15th Street**, the site at **1 Ocean Drive**, and a **40-year lease**
- Contracts signed in **1985**

So while Fort Lauderdale was tearing itself apart, Jack was already building the next empire 30 miles south.

## The Miami Beach Pivot

### The Context: What Miami Beach Was in 1984
- The American Riviera of the 1950s had decayed into a quiet, declining community
- Median population age: **72 years old**
- Catering to retired seniors living on social security
- Crime-ridden — drug addicts sleeping on Ocean Drive, paraphernalia on the beach
- Boarded-up buildings, rundown hotels
- The city was desperate for someone to bring it back

### What Jack Did
**1985**: Contracts signed. Began promotional tours.
- Toured Northeastern college campuses (same playbook as Fort Lauderdale)
- Created press stunts in freezing northern cities — flew in palm trees, sand, even Miami Vice actors
- Partnered with the City Commission, Hotel Association, and Visitors & Convention Bureau

**1986**: First Spring Break activation on Miami Beach:
- Toga parties on the sand
- A **man-made ski mountain in Lummus Park** — guests skiing in bathing suits (Budweiser sponsored, used shaved ice, an Olympic silver medalist participated)
- Beachside concerts
- Professional volleyball courts
- Generated international press coverage

**November 1988**: Penrod's Beach Club opened at **1 Ocean Drive**:
- **$4 million** investment in a derelict neighborhood
- Three-story, **35,000 square foot** entertainment complex
- Sports bars, seafood restaurants, nightclub, live music stages, volleyball courts, pool
- **50,000+ people at the opening**
- "It was out-of-control amazing"

### The Results
- Used same promotional techniques from his entire career — "and they worked"
- Schedule of special events: volleyball tournaments, Super Bowl parties with screens on the sand, triathlons, pro-sail competitions, concerts (including **Luciano Pavarotti**)
- "We had lines for blocks"
- Within 20 years, Miami Beach's median population age dropped from **72 to 40**
- Jack Penrod is widely credited with single-handedly sparking the revitalization of South Beach

## The Irony

Fort Lauderdale's city fathers drove out the one entrepreneur who would go on to transform Miami Beach from a crime-ridden retirement community into one of the most iconic destinations in the world. They chased away the golden goose — and Miami Beach caught it.

The same aggressive marketing, the same instinct for spectacle, the same ability to move a crowd — everything that made Jack successful in Fort Lauderdale was exactly what he brought to Miami Beach. The only difference was that Miami Beach *wanted* him.

## The Evolution: Penrod's → Café Nikki → Nikki Beach

- Penrod's Miami Beach operated successfully for **nearly a decade**
- **December 24, 1997**: Jack's 18-year-old daughter **Nicole Penrod** was killed by a drunk driver, weeks before high school graduation
- Jack didn't want to mourn her — he wanted to celebrate her life
- He **removed the pool** at Penrod's and built a garden by hand (he and Lucia planted every plant themselves)
- Created **Café Nikki** — "a magical garden by the sea, almost like a sanctuary"
- Tagline: **"Celebration of Life"**
- Madonna, Michael Douglas, Harrison Ford came and encouraged him to expand
- **1998**: Expanded onto the beach → **Nikki Beach** was born
- Philosophy: "Tell Only Your Best Friends" — no advertising, trickle-down from influential clientele
- White sunbeds, flowing fabrics, soft color palette — all in Nicole's memory
- Grew organically at guests' request → Saint-Tropez (2002), St. Barth, Marbella, eventually **13 beach clubs and 4 hotels** across 10 countries
- Jack owned **100% of the company with zero debt**
- Was offered "many millions" to sell but refused: "I still feel like a caretaker for my daughter"

## Jack Penrod — Key Personal Details

- Born **June 25, 1939** in Columbus, Ohio
- One of **7 children**, family on welfare
- At age 12, told his mother: "Don't worry, one day we would be rich"
- Started working at age **9** (newspapers, bakery pans)
- Graduated high school and **hitchhiked to the Florida Keys**, knocked on doors until he found a job
- Key Colony Cabana Club by day, dishwasher at the Shamrock by night
- Worked at the **Greenbrier** hotel in West Virginia
- Met a **Rockefeller** who hired him as a companion/big brother to her 10-year-old son — "That's when I decided it wasn't bad to be rich"
- Started McDonald's in **1961** as a line cook for **85 cents/hour**
- Promoted to manager in **3 days**
- Took his location from $90K/year to **$440K/year** — Ray Kroc flew down to see what "this kid" was doing
- Pink Jeep delivering burgers to college dorms
- Wrote ***76 Ways to Promote Your McDonald's***
- Ray Kroc told him: **"You do any damn thing you want"**
- Partnered with Harold Hill, acquired **16 McDonald's** in 7 years
- Sold in **1975** for approximately **$10 million**
- Founded **Wuv's International** (hamburger chain) — filed Chapter 11 in **1981**
- Then pivoted to entertainment → Penrod's Beach Club
- Published autobiography: ***One Grand Adventure: Becoming Rich in Business, Family and Life*** (2022, Fedd Books, 184 pages)
- **April 17, 2012** named "Jack Penrod Day" by Miami-Dade County and City of Miami Beach
- Died **February 3, 2025** from cancer in Fort Lauderdale, age 85
